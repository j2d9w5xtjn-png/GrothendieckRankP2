## 0. Executive summary for the next agent

The computational evidence has stopped changing the shape of the problem. The finite-ring ladder is now very strong, but no finite ladder can prove the general theorem by itself. The right target is no longer “find another ring where \([4]^\#=0\).” The right target is:

\[
\boxed{
S'(A/R):\quad \phi(I)\subseteq \mathfrak m(\ker\phi\cap I),
\qquad \phi=[2]^\#=\mu\circ\Delta.
}
\]

Here \((R,\mathfrak m,k)\) is an Artin local base of residue characteristic 2, \(A=R\oplus I\) is the rank-4 coordinate algebra in the remaining local killed-by-2 branch, and \(\phi=[2]^\#\) is the coordinate pullback of multiplication-by-2 on the augmentation ideal.

The existing theory file already proves that \(S'(A/R)\) implies all socle-line lifts are killed by 4. Therefore a universal proof of \(S'\), or of its propagation along socle steps, closes the remaining order-4 case. The new proof push refines this as follows:

\[
\boxed{
S'\text{ is the divided }[4]\text{ identity.}
}
\]

In a principal equal-character ring \(R=k[t]/t^{N+1}\), the special fiber killed-by-2 condition gives \(\phi(I)\subseteq tI\). Define

\[
D_t\phi(x)=\phi(y),\qquad ty=\phi(x).
\]

This is well-defined. Then

\[
S'(A/R)\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad D_t\phi=0,
\]

whereas ordinary killed-by-4 gives only

\[
\phi^2=tD_t\phi=0.
\]

Thus the remaining theorem is stronger than killed-by-4 at the same base. It asks for the division of \([4]^\#\) by the maximal-ideal generator to vanish.

For non-principal \(\mathfrak m=(a_1,\ldots,a_e)\), the same statement becomes a Koszul saturation identity:

\[
\phi(I)\subseteq (a_1,
\ldots,a_e)(\ker\phi\cap I).
\]

The FatPoint3/xy `unknown` is exactly the solver struggling with this syzygy-coset/Koszul formulation, not evidence of a counterexample.

The next hand proof should try to prove the divided identity using only the minimal axiom package isolated by `minassoc.m2`:

1. associativity of \(\mu\),
2. multiplicativity of \(\Delta\),
3. counitality and the rank-4 local killed-by-2 fiber shape.

Coassociativity and antipodes are probably not where the identity lives, at least at the first nontrivial depth.

## 2. Notation and exact local branch

Work in the remaining branch only.

* \((R,\mathfrak m,k)\) is an Artin local ring, residue characteristic 2.
* \(A\) is finite free of rank 4 over \(R\), with counit \(\varepsilon:A\to R\).
* \(I=\ker\varepsilon\), so \(A=R\oplus I\) as an \(R\)-module.
* \(\mu:A\otimes A\to A\) is multiplication.
* \(\Delta:A\to A\otimes A\) is a counital multiplicative comultiplication.
* \(\phi=[2]^\#=\mu\circ\Delta:I\to I\).

The special fiber is killed by 2, so

\[
\bar\phi=0,
\qquad\text{hence}\qquad
\phi(I)\subseteq \mathfrak m I.
\]

Set

\[
K=\ker\phi\cap I.
\]

The desired strengthening is

\[
S'(A/R):\qquad \phi(I)\subseteq \mathfrak mK.
\]

Ordinary killed-by-4 is

\[
\phi^2=0.
\]

Since \(\phi(I)\subseteq\mathfrak m I\), killed-by-4 gives

\[
\phi(I)\subseteq K\cap\mathfrak m I.
\]

But \(S'\) asks for the stronger saturation statement

\[
\phi(I)\subseteq\mathfrak mK.
\]

The quotient measuring the gap is

\[
\mathcal O(A/R)
=
\frac{K\cap\mathfrak m I}{\mathfrak mK}.
\]

The obstruction map is

\[
\omega_A:I\longrightarrow \mathcal O(A/R),
\qquad
x\longmapsto [\phi(x)].
\]

Then

\[
S'(A/R)\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \omega_A=0.
\]

A counterexample to \(S'\) is therefore not necessarily a counterexample to killed-by-4. It is a failure of \(\mathfrak m\)-saturation inside the kernel of \(\phi\).

## 3. Minimal-counterexample reduction for \(S'\)

This is the cleanest way to think about the final obstruction.

Assume \(S'\) fails, and choose a counterexample \((R,A)\) with \(\ell(R)\) minimal. Choose a socle line \(M\subset\operatorname{Soc}(R)\), and set

\[
\bar R=R/M,
\qquad
\bar A=A\otimes_R\bar R.
\]

By minimality, \(S'(\bar A/\bar R)\) holds. The existing Theorem 7.1 mechanism in `THEORY_order4.md` then gives that every socle-line lift of \(\bar A\), including \(A\), is killed by 4. Therefore

\[
\phi^2=0
\]

on the minimal \(S'\)-counterexample.

So the minimal obstruction has the following form:

\[
\phi(I)\subseteq K\cap\mathfrak m I
\quad\text{but}\quad
\phi(I)\not\subseteq\mathfrak mK.
\]

Equivalently, some \(x\in I\) has

\[
[\phi(x)]\neq 0
\quad\text{in}\quad
\mathcal O(A/R)=\frac{K\cap\mathfrak m I}{\mathfrak mK}.
\]

This is important: a proof of the full theorem is not trying to show \(\phi^2=0\) in this minimal situation. That is already forced. It is trying to show that a certain cycle is divisible by \(\mathfrak m\) **inside cycles**.

As pure module theory this is false. For example, over \(k[t]/t^4\), take \(M=R\) and \(d=t^2\operatorname{id}\). Then \(d^2=0\), but

\[
d(M)=t^2M,
\qquad
\ker d=(t^2),
\qquad
t\ker d=(t^3),
\]

so \(d(M)\not\subseteq t\ker d\). The bialgebra identities must do real work.

## 4. Principal case: \(S'\) is divided \([4]\)

Let

\[
R=k[t]/t^{N+1}.
\]

Since the special fiber is killed by 2,

\[
\phi(I)\subseteq tI.
\]

For \(x\in I\), choose \(y\in I\) with

\[
ty=\phi(x).
\]

The choice of \(y\) is ambiguous by \(\operatorname{ann}(t)I=t^N I\). But \(\phi(I)\subseteq tI\), so

\[
\phi(t^N I)\subseteq t^{N+1}I=0.
\]

Thus the expression

\[
D_t\phi(x):=\phi(y)
\]

is well-defined.

Now:

\[
S'(A/R)
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
D_t\phi=0.
\]

Proof: \(S'\) says that for each \(x\), \(\phi(x)=tk\) for some \(k\in K=\ker\phi\cap I\). This is exactly saying that after dividing \(\phi(x)\) by \(t\), the result can be chosen in \(\ker\phi\), i.e. \(D_t\phi(x)=0\). The well-definedness above shows this does not depend on the chosen division.

Meanwhile,

\[
\phi^2(x)=\phi(ty)=t\phi(y)=tD_t\phi(x).
\]

Therefore

\[
[4]^\#=0
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\phi^2=0
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
 tD_t\phi=0,
\]

but the desired \(S'\) identity is the stronger assertion

\[
\boxed{D_t\phi=0.}
\]

This is why \(S'\)-checks are stronger than direct \([4]\)-checks. They prove one coefficient beyond killed-by-4.

### 4.1 Coefficient expansion

In equal characteristic, write

\[
\phi=t\psi_1+t^2\psi_2+\cdots+t^N\psi_N,
\]

with \(k\)-linear endomorphisms \(\psi_i\) of the special-fiber augmentation ideal. Then

\[
\frac{\phi}{t}=\psi_1+t\psi_2+\cdots+t^{N-1}\psi_N.
\]

Therefore

\[
D_t\phi
=
\phi\left(\frac{\phi}{t}\right)
=
\sum_{i,j\ge 1} t^{i+j-1}\,\psi_i\psi_j.
\]

Equivalently, the coefficient of \(t^r\) is

\[
\sum_{i+j=r+1}\psi_i\psi_j.
\]

Thus \(S'\) is equivalent to the family of identities

\[
\boxed{
\sum_{i+j=n}\psi_i\psi_j=0,
\qquad 2\le n\le N+1.
}
\]

The first identity is

\[
\psi_1^2=0.
\]

This is exactly the first nontrivial identity proved by the \(\epsilon^3\) computations. The later ones are higher polarized / Massey-corrected versions:

\[
\psi_1\psi_2+\psi_2\psi_1=0,
\]

\[
\psi_1\psi_3+\psi_2^2+\psi_3\psi_1=0,
\]

and so on.

### 4.2 Why naive polarization is insufficient

A tempting shortcut is:

1. `minassoc` proves \(\psi^2=0\) for every first-order symbol \(\psi\);
2. polarize to get \(\psi_i\psi_j+\psi_j\psi_i=0\);
3. conclude all coefficient sums vanish.

This is not currently valid as written.

The issue is that \(\psi_i\) for \(i>1\) need not be an arbitrary first-order symbol. It is the \(i\)-th coefficient of the nonlinear expression \(\mu\circ\Delta\), and it may contain lower-order correction terms. In deformation-theoretic language, these are Massey-product corrections.

So the missing lemma is not simply “first-order symbols square to zero.” It is:

\[
\boxed{
\text{the higher corrected symbols satisfy the same divided square-zero identities.}
}
\]

This is the coefficient form of \(S'\)-propagation.

## 5. Non-principal case: Koszul / syzygy-coset form

Let

\[
\mathfrak m=(a_1,\ldots,a_e).
\]

Define

\[
\partial:I^e\longrightarrow I,
\qquad
(y_1,
\ldots,y_e)\longmapsto\sum_{\nu=1}^e a_\nu y_\nu.
\]

Then \(\mathfrak mI=\operatorname{im}\partial\). For \(x\in I\), choose a division tuple

\[
y=(y_1,
\ldots,y_e)\in I^e,
\qquad
\partial y=\phi(x).
\]

Changing the division tuple changes \(y\) by an element of

\[
\operatorname{Syz}(a;I)=\ker\partial.
\]

Now \(S'\) at \(x\) says there exists a division tuple with every component in \(K\):

\[
\exists k=(k_1,
\ldots,k_e)\in K^e
\quad\text{such that}\quad
\partial k=\phi(x).
\]

Equivalently,

\[
\boxed{
y_0\in K^e+\operatorname{Syz}(a;I)
}
\]

for one, hence every, chosen division tuple \(y_0\) with \(\partial y_0=\phi(x)\).

Thus the exact obstruction class is

\[
[y_0]\in
\frac{I^e}{K^e+\operatorname{Syz}(a;I)}.
\]

This is the non-principal version of division by \(t\). It is why the FatPoint3/xy case is harder than the principal cases: the division choices form a genuine syzygy coset, not a single ann\((t)\)-coset.

### 5.1 Relation to \(\mathcal O(A/R)\)

The intrinsic obstruction quotient is still

\[
\mathcal O(A/R)=\frac{K\cap\mathfrak m I}{\mathfrak mK}.
\]

The syzygy-coset formulation is the computational way of testing whether the class of \(\phi(x)\in K\cap\mathfrak mI\) lies in \(\mathfrak mK\). In other words,

\[
[\phi(x)]=0\in\mathcal O(A/R)
\]

if and only if a division tuple can be chosen inside \(K^e\).

### 5.2 Caution: no principal defect shortcut

In the principal case, one can divide by \(t\), apply \(\phi\), and get a well-defined defect \(D_t\phi\). In the non-principal case, applying \(\phi\) to a division tuple depends nontrivially on the syzygy shift. The free syzygy components are not killed by \(\phi\) in general.

So do not try to port the principal defect shortcut blindly. The right object is the coset

\[
\partial^{-1}(\phi(x))=y_0+\operatorname{Syz}(a;I)
\]

and the question whether it intersects \(K^e\).

## 6. Desired theorem statements

### 6.1 Main desired theorem: universal \(S'\)

**Theorem X, desired.**  
Let \((R,\mathfrak m,k)\) be Artin local with residue characteristic 2. Let \(A=R\oplus I\) be a finite free rank-4 commutative \(R\)-algebra with counital multiplicative comultiplication \(\Delta\). Assume the special fiber is local and killed by 2. Put

\[
\phi=[2]^\#=\mu\circ\Delta:I\to I.
\]

Then

\[
\phi(I)\subseteq\mathfrak m(\ker\phi\cap I).
\]

Equivalently, \(S'(A/R)\) holds universally.

A still stronger version, suggested by `minassoc`, is:

> The inclusion above is a formal consequence of associativity of \(\mu\), multiplicativity of \(\Delta\), counitality, rank 4, and the killed-by-2 local special-fiber shape. Coassociativity and antipodes are not needed for the identity.

### 6.2 Principal divided-\([4]\) theorem

**Theorem X\(_{\mathrm{prin}}\), desired.**  
Let \(R=k[t]/t^{N+1}\). Under the same rank-4/killed-by-2 hypotheses,

\[
D_t\phi=0,
\qquad D_t\phi(x)=\phi(y),\ ty=\phi(x).
\]

Equivalently,

\[
\sum_{i+j=n}\psi_i\psi_j=0
\qquad
(2\le n\le N+1)
\]

for \(\phi=\sum_{i=1}^N t^i\psi_i\).

### 6.3 Non-principal Koszul theorem

**Theorem X\(_{\mathrm{Koszul}}\), desired.**  
Let \(\mathfrak m=(a_1,\ldots,a_e)\). For every \(x\in I\), every division tuple

\[
y_0\in I^e,
\qquad
\sum a_\nu y_{0,\nu}=\phi(x),
\]

has class zero in

\[
\frac{I^e}{K^e+\operatorname{Syz}(a;I)}.
\]

Equivalently,

\[
\phi(I)\subseteq(a_1,
\ldots,a_e)K.
\]

## 7. Conditional proof of the order-4 theorem from Theorem X

This is the proof that should be written immediately if Theorem X is proved.

**Conditional theorem.**  
Assume Theorem X. Then every finite locally free group scheme of order 4 is killed by 4.

**Proof sketch.**

3. For \(\ell(R)>1\), choose a socle-line quotient

   \[
   R\twoheadrightarrow \bar R=R/M.
   \]

4. By induction and the existing reductions, the relevant quotient object over \(\bar R\) is in the killed-by-4 branch. By Theorem X, \(S'(\bar A/\bar R)\) holds:

   \[

6. This closes the induction in the remaining branch. The other branches were already reduced away. Therefore every order-4 finite locally free group scheme is killed by 4.

## 8. Best current hand-proof strategy

The proof target should be an explicit universal syzygy.

At the principal level, seek an identity of the form

\[
\boxed{
D_t\phi
=
\sum_\alpha c_\alpha\operatorname{Assoc}_\alpha
+
\sum_\beta d_\beta\operatorname{Compat}_\beta.
}
\]

Here:

* \(D_t\phi=\phi(\phi/t)\) is the divided \([4]\) obstruction;
* \(\operatorname{Assoc}_\alpha\) are the coordinate associativity equations;
* \(\operatorname{Compat}_\beta\) are the coordinate equations expressing multiplicativity of \(\Delta\);
* the cofactors \(c_\alpha,d_\beta\) are universal polynomials in the structure constants.

In the non-principal case, replace division by \(t\) with the Koszul/syzygy-coset complex. The desired identity becomes membership of the \(S'\)-obstruction class in the module generated by associativity and compatibility equations.

### 8.1 Why this is plausible

`minassoc.m2` has already proved exactly the first nontrivial instance:

* base \(k[\epsilon]/\epsilon^3\), arbitrary \(\mathbb F_2\)-algebra \(k\);
* both killed-by-2 local fiber shapes;
* no coassociativity;
* no fiber2 hypothesis;
* all \([4]^\#\) coefficients lie in the ideal generated by associativity + \(\Delta\)-multiplicativity.

In \(k[\epsilon]/\epsilon^3\), ordinary \([4]^\#=0\) is the same as the first divided identity \(\psi_1^2=0\), because

\[
\phi=\epsilon\psi_1+\epsilon^2\psi_2,
\qquad
\phi^2=\epsilon^2\psi_1^2.
\]

So the first divided syzygy exists. The goal is to identify the pattern and prove it to all orders.

### 8.2 What to mine from certificates

The most valuable certificate is not a raw certificate for \([4]=0\) over another finite ring. It is a certificate for the divided identity.

Mine `minassoc.m2` first, not the older `certs.m2`, because `minassoc` has already stripped the problem to the minimal axiom package.

For each fiber, try to extract and simplify the cofactors expressing

\[
\psi_1^2
\]

in the ideal generated by associativity and compatibility equations. Look for repeated patterns:

* compatibility applied to products in the augmentation ideal;
* associativity corrections moving parentheses before applying \(\mu\circ\Delta\);
* terms showing that \(\mu\Delta\mu\Delta\) factors through a compatibility commutator;
* cancellations that use characteristic 2 but not coassociativity.

The human proof may be a short string-diagram identity. Try writing \(\phi^2\) as

\[
\mu\Delta\mu\Delta
=
\mu(\mu\otimes\mu)(1\otimes\tau\otimes1)(\Delta\otimes\Delta)\Delta,
\]

then isolate the terms that survive after subtracting the special-fiber killed-by-2 part and dividing by \(t\). Because `minassoc` says coassociativity is not needed, avoid using

\[
(\Delta\otimes1)\Delta=(1\otimes\Delta)\Delta
\]

unless the identity truly requires it at higher order.

### 8.3 Inductive coefficient route

For principal equal-character bases, a promising induction is:

1. Assume \(S'\) known modulo \(t^N\).
2. Lift to \(t^{N+1}\).
3. The new defect \(D_t\phi\) is supported in the top layer \(t^N I\).
4. Identify this top-layer defect as a first-order divided obstruction attached to the square-zero extension

   \[
   0\to t^N R\to R/(t^{N+1})\to R/(t^N)\to0.
   \]

5. Use the first-order syzygy pattern, but now relative to a base where \(\bar\phi\) may not vanish; this is where Massey corrections enter.

This is exactly where the current proof attempt stalls. The known first-order theorem handles deformations of a killed-by-2 fiber. The induction step needs a **relative** first-order theorem over a base satisfying \(S'\), not necessarily one with \(\bar\phi=0\).

A good formulation of the missing relative lemma is:

> If \(S'(A/R)\) holds and \(A'\) is a socle-line lift over \(R'\), then the top-layer divided \([4]\) defect of \(A'\) vanishes.

This is stronger than Theorem 7.1, which gives only top-layer ordinary \([4]\) vanishing.

### 8.4 Bockstein/Massey interpretation

The map \(\phi:I\to I\) with \(\phi(I)\subseteq\mathfrak mI\) gives a filtered endomorphism. Dividing \(\phi\) by a maximal-ideal generator, or by the Koszul complex on generators of \(\mathfrak m\), is a Bockstein operation. Then \(S'\) says this Bockstein lands in cycles.

The obstruction \(\omega_A\) is a secondary operation:

\[
\omega_A(x)=[\phi(x)]\in\frac{K\cap\mathfrak m I}{\mathfrak mK}.
\]

The coefficient identities

\[
\sum_{i+j=n}\psi_i\psi_j=0
\]

are exactly the vanishing of the corresponding Massey products.

This language may help organize the proof, but beware: ordinary filtered-module Bockstein formalism does not prove the result. The module counterexample in §3 shows that the bialgebra identities are essential.
